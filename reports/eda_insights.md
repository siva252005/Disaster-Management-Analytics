# Phase 4: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) Report

## Dataset Overview

* Total Records: 14,455
* Total Features: 13
* Time Period: 1970–2021
* Countries Covered: 228
* Continents Covered: 5

---

# 1. Disaster Type Distribution

### Key Findings

* Floods are the most frequent disasters globally.
* Storms are the second most common disaster type.
* Epidemics and earthquakes are also significant contributors.
* Rare disasters such as impacts, animal accidents, and glacial lake outbursts have very few occurrences.

### Insight

Hydrological and meteorological disasters dominate global disaster events.

---

# 2. Year-wise Disaster Trend

### Key Findings

* Disaster occurrences increased steadily from 1970 onward.
* Significant growth occurred after 1990.
* Peak disaster frequency occurred during the early 2000s.
* Although the number of disasters slightly declined after 2010, the frequency remains high.

### Insight

The increasing trend indicates growing climate variability and improved disaster reporting systems.

# 6. Severity Analysis

### Key Findings

* Most disasters belong to the Low severity category.
* Medium severity events are comparatively fewer.
* High-severity disasters are rare but extremely destructive.

### Insight

A small number of severe disasters contribute disproportionately to global losses and casualties.

---

# 7. Deaths by Disaster Type

### Key Findings

1. Earthquakes caused the highest number of deaths.
2. Storms rank second.
3. Droughts rank third.
4. Floods also contribute significantly.
5. Epidemics and extreme temperatures have moderate impacts.

### Insight

Although floods occur most frequently, earthquakes are responsible for the highest mortality.

---

# 8. Economic Loss by Disaster Type

### Key Findings

1. Storms caused the highest economic losses.
2. Floods rank second.
3. Earthquakes rank third.
4. Droughts and wildfires contribute moderate losses.

### Insight

Meteorological disasters have the greatest economic impact worldwide.

# 10. Correlation Analysis

### Observations

* Affected population and Total Affected are perfectly correlated.
* Deaths and Injured have weak positive correlation.
* Economic losses have low correlation with casualties.
* Human impact and financial impact are not strongly related.

### Insight

Highly destructive events do not necessarily cause the greatest economic losses, and vice versa.

---

# 11. Decade-wise Trend

### Key Findings

* 1970s had the fewest disasters.
* Disaster frequency increased steadily in the 1980s and 1990s.
* The 2000s recorded the highest number of disasters.
* The 2010s maintained high disaster activity.
* The lower count in the 2020s is due to partial decade data (2020–2021).

### Insight

Global disaster occurrences have increased significantly over the past five decades.

---

# Overall Business Insights

* Floods are the most common disasters globally.
* Earthquakes are responsible for the largest number of deaths.
* Storms generate the highest economic losses.
* Asia is the most affected continent.
* China and the United States experience the highest number of disasters.
* Bangladesh recorded the highest cumulative deaths.
* The United States suffered the highest economic losses.
* Disaster frequency has increased substantially since the 1970s.
* Most disasters are classified as low severity, while a few high-severity events account for major human and economic impacts.

---

# Conclusion

Historical disaster data reveals increasing disaster occurrences and significant geographical variations. Floods and storms dominate global disaster frequency, while earthquakes are responsible for the highest mortality. Asia remains the most vulnerable continent. These findings can assist governments and disaster management authorities in improving preparedness, risk assessment, and resource allocation strategies.
